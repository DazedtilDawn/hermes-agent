# Phase 3 Failure Signatures

These are not hypothetical concerns. They are practical warning signs. If you observe them, assume erosion is already underway.

## 1. Class1 starts absorbing convenience
Failure signature:
Actions enter class1 because they are familiar, low-friction, or usually safe rather than because they are narrowly reversible and explicitly pre-approved.
Interpretation:
Class1 is no longer a constrained hygiene lane. It is becoming a fast path for trusted action.

## 2. Reversibility becomes rhetorical
Failure signature:
Reversibility is described informally, inferred from operator confidence, or justified by prior success rather than specified concretely in the playbook and artifact chain.
Interpretation:
The system is replacing defined rollback conditions with intuition.

## 3. Verifier outputs begin to unblock action in practice
Failure signature:
Downstream actors begin treating validated, low-risk, or well-scoped verifier outputs as sufficient reason to proceed even without formal approval.
Interpretation:
The verifier has acquired de facto authority without formal constitutional change.

## 4. Novelty rates fall while operational diversity rises
Failure signature:
The system classifies fewer cases as novel even as incident variety, edge cases, or environmental variance increases.
Interpretation:
Novelty is being tuned downward to preserve throughput.

## 5. Boundary uncertainty stops causing escalation
Failure signature:
Ambiguous cases are routinely forced into existing categories rather than escalated because escalation is seen as costly, noisy, or slow.
Interpretation:
The system is optimizing for flow over caution.

## 6. Owner review becomes queue clearing
Failure signature:
Queue items are acknowledged quickly, resolved quickly, or dismissed quickly, but with weak differentiation, thin judgment context, or little visible decision reasoning.
Interpretation:
Owner review is still present procedurally but is degrading into ritual.

## 7. Queue health is measured by emptiness
Failure signature:
The system treats shorter queues, faster clearing, or lower escalation volume as evidence of governance quality without asking whether the decisions were correct.
Interpretation:
Governance is being evaluated as workflow efficiency rather than control quality.

## 8. Protected-surface touches get reframed instead of blocked
Failure signature:
Protected changes are relabeled as metadata, operational adjustments, incidental touches, or non-constitutional edits in order to avoid stronger controls.
Interpretation:
The protected-surface boundary is being softened through language before being softened in policy.

## 9. Temporary exceptions survive contact with reality
Failure signature:
Emergency paths, one-off overrides, or temporary allowances remain available after the incident that justified them has passed.
Interpretation:
Exception handling is becoming precedent formation.

## 10. Governance incidents accumulate without changing anything
Failure signature:
The same governance incident families recur, but policies, playbooks, thresholds, or escalation rules do not materially improve in response.
Interpretation:
Incidents are being stored, not learned from.

## 11. Notifications are treated as proof of control
Failure signature:
Teams begin talking as if alerts sent, messages delivered, or queue notifications acknowledged are evidence that governance worked.
Interpretation:
Communication is being mistaken for constitutional enforcement.

## 12. Audit language becomes stronger than control language
Failure signature:
More effort goes into explaining, summarizing, and documenting why an action was acceptable after the fact than into proving it was properly constrained before execution.
Interpretation:
The system is drifting from pre-action discipline toward post-action justification.

## 13. Definitions stay fixed on paper but loosen in use
Failure signature:
Terms like reversible, novel, protected, incidental, and pre-approved remain formally unchanged while their practical interpretation expands over time.
Interpretation:
Vocabulary drift is dissolving the constitution without rewriting it.

## 14. Interfaces imply authority they do not officially grant
Failure signature:
Verifier recommendations, queue UI language, incident labels, or operator affordances begin to suggest that certain steps are effectively approved when they are only assessed or routed.
Interpretation:
The interface is teaching users the wrong constitutional model.

## 15. Governance becomes easier to explain than to enforce
Failure signature:
The system can always produce a reasonable narrative for why a decision happened, but it becomes harder to identify the hard constraint that forced the decision before action.
Interpretation:
The design is preserving the appearance of legitimacy while losing its control mechanics.

## Response rule

If one of these signatures appears, do not localize it as a minor implementation bug. Treat it as evidence that the governance layer is being reinterpreted under pressure.

## Immediate questions to ask

- What authority boundary just softened?
- What escalation trigger just got downgraded?
- What appearance of discipline is being preserved while real constraint weakens?
- Is this a one-off anomaly, or the start of a normalized path?

## Bottom line

Phase 3 fails when the system still looks governed while becoming easier to widen, excuse, or bypass. The point of watching for failure signatures is to detect erosion early enough that convenience does not become the real constitution.
